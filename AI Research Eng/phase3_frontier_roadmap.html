How to read Nielsen & Chuang: Read a section, close the book, open a blank Qiskit notebook, and implement what you just read. Shor's algorithm on paper is abstract. Shor's algorithm running on a quantum simulator — even incorrectly at first — is concrete. The book + Qiskit loop is the most effective way to process this material.

Variational Quantum Circuits — the NISQ-era workhorse
Months 29–30 · Bridge to QML
▼
▶
PennyLane documentation + demos — pennylane.ai PrimaryFree
PennyLane by Xanadu is the PyTorch of quantum ML — automatic differentiation through quantum circuits. Their demo gallery (pennylane.ai/qml/demonstrations) has 100+ free tutorials covering VQE, QAOA, quantum neural networks, quantum kernels, barren plateaus, and quantum chemistry. This is your primary QML framework — it integrates directly with PyTorch and TensorFlow, letting you build hybrid classical-quantum models in familiar syntax.
📄
Variational Quantum Algorithms — Cerezo et al. 2021 (Nature Reviews Physics) Free arXiv:2012.09265
The definitive review paper on variational quantum algorithms. 50 pages covering VQE, QAOA, quantum neural networks, barren plateaus (the vanishing gradient problem of QML), and hardware noise. Read after completing the Qiskit textbook. This paper will become your most-cited reference when talking to quantum researchers and clients.
The barren plateau problem is quantum's vanishing gradient: Just as deep neural networks suffered from vanishing gradients before batch norm and residual connections, variational quantum circuits suffer from barren plateaus — gradients exponentially vanishing with circuit depth. Understanding this problem, its causes, and current mitigations (local cost functions, layerwise training, quantum natural gradient) is what separates a serious QML researcher from someone who just runs tutorials.

Your first paper — the research question framework
Months 30–36 · Target: arXiv preprint
▼
📖
The three paths to a first paper Framework
Path A — Reproduction + extension: Reproduce a paper's results exactly, then change one variable (dataset, architecture, training regime) and measure the effect. This is the easiest first paper and is publishable as a reproducibility study or ablation analysis.

Path B — Application to new domain: Take a technique from one domain (e.g. QML kernel methods) and apply it to a new problem (e.g. Indian financial time series). Novel application = novel contribution.

Path C — Negative result: Rigorously show that a hyped technique does not work under specific conditions. Negative results are undervalued and undersubmitted — they are publishable and important.
▶
Workshop papers — NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR workshops Free to submit
Full conference papers (NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR main track) have ~20% acceptance rates and require years of iteration. Workshop papers at the same conferences have 40–60% acceptance rates and are reviewed in 4–6 weeks. Your first submission should target a workshop — specifically: QuantumTech workshop at NeurIPS, QTML conference (Quantum Techniques in Machine Learning), or the Finance + ML workshop tracks. One workshop paper changes your professional positioning permanently.
Target: workshop first → main track later
The co-authorship path: Find 1–2 researchers whose work you've been following and reproducing. Email them with a specific, detailed observation about their paper — not "I loved your work" but "I reproduced your experiment and noticed X discrepancy, which I traced to Y, and I think it implies Z." That email, if thoughtful, has a 30–40% response rate. A conversation that starts there can become a co-authorship. This is how most non-PhD researchers enter the research community.

1
Complex analysis + Hilbert spaces — quantum math
🔴 PRIMARY · Months 25–30
▼
📖
Visual Complex Analysis — Tristan Needham Primary Book #1Book
The most beautiful mathematics book of the last 30 years. Needham rebuilds complex analysis using geometric intuition rather than algebraic manipulation. Every theorem has a picture. After reading this, complex numbers feel as natural as real numbers — which matters because quantum amplitudes are complex, quantum gates are unitary complex matrices, and the Fourier transform (central to quantum algorithms) is complex analysis in disguise. Do not skip this book.
📖
Introduction to Hilbert Space — Young Book · Supplementary
Quantum states live in Hilbert spaces. After Axler's finite-dimensional linear algebra, Young extends the same ideas to infinite-dimensional spaces — which is the mathematical setting of quantum mechanics and functional analysis. Read Ch1–5. The inner product, completeness, and orthonormal basis chapters directly support your quantum computing work.
The connection you will feel: When you study Hilbert spaces, you will notice that the mathematics is identical to the mathematics of kernel methods in ML (Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces — RKHS). This is not a coincidence. It is the same underlying structure. This is a Von Neumann moment — two fields sharing one mathematical skeleton. Write it down in Obsidian when you feel it. It will become the opening paragraph of a paper.

Group theory + representation theory — the deep structure
Months 30–36 · Connects quantum to physics to ML
▼
📖
Visual Group Theory — Nathan Carter PrimaryBook + free online supplements
Group theory is the mathematics of symmetry. It underlies quantum mechanics (symmetry groups of physical systems), chemistry (molecular symmetry), and increasingly ML (equivariant neural networks, geometric deep learning). Carter's visual approach makes groups intuitive before making them rigorous. After this book: symmetry in quantum circuits, equivariant architectures in ML, and Lie groups in physics all become connected.
📄
Geometric Deep Learning — Bronstein et al. 2021 Free arXiv:2104.13478
The "proto-book" of geometric deep learning — unifying CNNs, GNNs, Transformers, and LSTMs under one group-theoretic framework. 150 pages free on arXiv. This is a frontier area connecting your ML depth to the group theory you're studying. Understanding this paper positions you for research at the intersection of differential geometry, group theory, and deep learning — exactly where the next wave of theoretical ML advances will come from.
